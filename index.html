Real-world violations introduce error:
Trap-shyness — previously caught rabbits avoid traps → fewer recaptures → overestimates N.
Marking stress — ear-tagging may injure or stress animals, reducing marked individuals' survival.
Open population — births, deaths, or migration between samples distort the ratio.
Patchy distribution — marked rabbits clustering in one area biases recapture rates.
Small m bias — tiny m values make N̂ extremely sensitive to random error.

Concept Review
1
First Capture (n₁)
Rabbits are caught with live traps. Each individual receives an ear tag and is released unharmed. The tag must be clearly visible but must not impair movement or attract predators.
2
Mixing Period
A waiting period lets tagged rabbits re-integrate randomly. Too short a wait causes spatial bias — marked individuals cluster near the release point.
3
Second Capture (n₂ & m)
A second sample is taken. Each rabbit is checked for an ear tag. The proportion of tagged (m) mirrors the proportion in the whole population — this is the key ratio.
4
Calculating N
Since m/n₂ = n₁/N, rearranging gives:
N = (n₁ × n₂) ÷ mMore tagged rabbits in the 2nd sample → smaller population estimate.
